It may also be used consistent with the principles of fair use under the copyright law.BACKGROUND:Despite national guidelines on appropriate antibiotic therapy, there is wide variation in antibiotic decision-making for children with community-acquired pneumonia. This study sought to determine prevalence and factors associated with guideline-concordant antibiotic use in children presenting with pneumonia to the emergency department (ED).METHODS:We enrolled children &lt;18 years presenting to the ED at two US children’s hospitals between Sept. 2017 and May 2019 with clinical and radiographic pneumonia. The primary outcome was guideline-concordant antibiotic use as defined by the 2011 Infectious Diseases Society of America pediatric pneumonia guideline and local expert consensus.. Outcomes included proportion of antibiotic use and proportion of guideline concordant treatment. We used multivariable logistic regression models to determine associations of comorbidities and functional limitations, clinical findings, and radiographic characteristics with overall antibiotic use and guideline-concordant treatment.RESULTS:Among 772 included children, 573 received antibiotics (74.2%) and 441 (57.1%) received guideline-concordant antibiotic treatment. Antibiotic initiation was less likely in those with interstitial findings on chest radiograph (aOR 0.14, 95% CI 0.07, 0.25) and negative or not performed viral testing (aOR 0.39, 95% CI, 0.24, 0.65). Guideline-concordant treatment was more likely in those with chest indrawing (aOR 2.22, 95% CI 1.34, 3.66) and less likely in those with clinically significant effusion (aOR 0.21, 95% CI 0.06, 0.76).CONCLUSIONS:Among children presenting to the ED with pneumonia, &gt;40% received treatment inconsistent with guideline recommendations. Though differentiating microbial etiologies is often challenging, inappropriate antibiotic use is common and not without consequence, including promotion of antimicrobial resistance and important adverse drug events, with a rate of 10.5 antibiotic-associated ED visits per 10,000 prescriptions) in one stud.7 Narrow-spectrum beta-lactam therapy (e.g., amoxicillin or ampicillin) is recommended when antibiotics are needed8, but broader spectrum antibiotics are often substituted.1,9Inappropriate antibiotic use is associated with worse outcomes, including treatment failure, increased costs, adverse drug events, and promotion of multidrug resistance.8,10–14 Safe reductions of antibiotic initiation and improved antibiotic selection are central to improving care for children with pneumonia. While some variation in care is expected when evidence is lacking, concordance with national guideline recommendations likely reflects standardization of appropriate antibiotic prescribing. Contemporary rates of guideline-concordant antibiotic initiation and selection, and factors associated with guideline-concordant prescribing, are not well characterized.11,15 Evidence suggests that fever, crackles on exam, receipt of antibiotics in the ED, and use of supplemental oxygen may be associated with antibiotic use in the inpatient setting, but relationship of these factor with guideline concordant antibiotic prescribing is not reported.15 The objective of this study was to determine prevalence and factors associated with antibiotic initiation and guideline-concordant antibiotic treatment in children with community-acquired pneumonia.METHODSStudy Design and PopulationThis analysis was nested within a prospective observational cohort of 1064 children with CAP presenting for emergency care at one of two U.S. children’s hospitals in the Southeast and Western United States over a 20-month period (Sept. 2017 – May 2019). The parent study was a prospective observational cohort designed to validate a series of pneumonia severity risk models. That study included children ages 6 months to &lt;18 years presenting to the ED with signs and symptoms of acute lower respiratory infection, radiographic evidence of pneumonia, and a clinician-confirmed diagnosis of pneumonia. That study included children ages 6 months to &lt;18 years presenting to the ED with signs and symptoms of acute lower respiratory infection, radiographic evidence of pneumonia, and a clinician-confirmed diagnosis of pneumonia.16 The study excluded children if they had a tracheostomy, cystic fibrosis, severe immunosuppression, hospitalization within the preceding 7 days, or a clear alternative diagnosis.16After informed consent, caregivers of enrolled children were interviewed to obtain the child’s past medical history, history of present illness, and other pertinent information using a standardized questionnaire. Following discharge, the electronic medical record was reviewed to collect details of the hospital course, including detailed antibiotic use data, disease-related complications, and outcomes. Children without information on prior antibiotic use were excluded. The study was approved by each institution’s Human Research Protections Program.OutcomeThe primary outcome was prevalence of guideline-concordant antibiotic use as defined by the 2011 Infectious Diseases Society of America consensus guideline for the management of pediatric community-acquired pneumonia and local expert consensus.8 Expert consensus was used to clarify any areas of uncertainty in guideline recommendations to avoid classifying empiric treatment strategies as discordant when evidence was unclear or lacking. For example, we considered no antibiotics to be concordant because this strategy is cited in the guideline and because we considered it very unlikely that clinicians would withhold antibiotics unless the likelihood for bacterial disease was low or very low. We also excluded use of macrolides from the concordance definitions because clinical equipoise exists regarding the use of macrolide therapy in the treatment of pneumonia in children.17,18Guideline concordant treatment emphasized narrow-spectrum beta-lactam antibiotics when appropriate, included alternative recommendations for severe diseases and complications, allowed for no antibiotic use when appropriate and incorporated location of care. Definitions for guideline-concordant antibiotic use for children discharged from the ED to the outpatient setting, admitted to the inpatient floor, admitted to the intensive care unit (ICU) without mechanical ventilation or shock and admitted to the ICU with mechanical ventilation or shock were applied (Supplemental Tables 1–4). For example, in those discharged from the ED, the guidelines recommend consideration of no antibiotics or narrow spectrum antibiotics but allow for use β-lactamase inhibitors if the patient had prior Beta-lactam use before evaluation. Therefore, our guideline concordant definition took into account scenarios where the patient had no prior beta-lactam use and separately for patients who did have prior beta-lactam use (Supplemental Table 1). In this described case of a patient with no prior beta-lactam use during the current illness, treatment with no antibiotics or amoxicillin would be deemed guideline concordant whereas treatment with a different antibiotics (such as amoxicillin clavulanate or ceftriaxone) would be deemed not-guideline concordant. As noted above, no consideration was given to use of macrolides in the outcome definition, based on expert consensus interpretation of clinical equipoise in their use and equivocal recommendation of use in the IDSA guidelines. For example, if a patient discharged from the ED received amoxicillin with or without a macrolide, the care was deemed guideline-concordant. Similarly, separate definitions were established based on combinations of location of care (ED discharge, inpatient floor, ICU without mechanical ventilation or shock, ICU with mechanical ventilation or shock), prior beta-lactam use, presence of moderate to large parapneumonic effusion or and presence of necrotizing/severe pneumonia. These scenarios encompass IDSA guideline recommendation on antibiotic use in children with different pneumonia severities with and without prior beta-lactam use.The proportion of children with guideline-concordant treatment was calculated by dividing the number of children with guideline-concordant antibiotic use by the total number of enrolled children. Secondary outcomes included prevalence of non-receipt of antibiotics, factors associated with initiation of any antibiotic during ED or hospitalization and factors associated with guideline concordant treatment among those who received an antibiotic.CovariatesCovariates included age, sex, self-identified race and ethnicity (non-Hispanic white, non-Hispanic black, Hispanic or Latino, other/unknown), insurance type (public, private, both, none, unknown/refused), number of ED visits within last 12 months, comorbidity and functional limitations, chest indrawing or retractions, tachypnea, and wheezing, radiograph findings (single lobar consolidation, multilobar, interstitial, mixed, unknown), clinically significant effusion (defined as presence of parapneumonic effusion requiring drainage), and whether a multiplex PCR respiratory pathogen panel was obtained. Respiratory pathogen panel included testing for common viral agents (adenovirus, coronavirus, metapneumovirus, rhinovirus/enterovirus, influenza, parainfluenza and respiratory syncytial virus) as well as atypical bacterial pathogens (bordetella parapertussis, bordetella pertussis, chlamydia pneumoniae and mycoplasma pneumoniae). Viral etiologies are common causes of pediatric pneumonia. We constructed a composite covariate of RPP negative/not obtained to better understand how identifying a viral etiology might associate with antibiotic use. Chest indrawing or retractions, tachypnea, and wheezing were determined by review of ED clinical documentation. Chest radiograph findings were based on initial chest radiograph and standardized radiographic interpretation was completed by a board-certified pediatric study radiologist at each study hospital. Race and ethnicity were included due to documented disparities in appropriate antibiotic prescribing by these characteristics.19Comorbidities included any condition present or expected to be present for 12 months or more and for which any of the following were true: (1) required daily medications; (2) required contact with specialty health care providers or frequent contact with a primary health care provider; or (3) limited the ability to perform age-appropriate activities or ability to interact with peers of the same age at least some of the time.20 Except for asthma, comorbidities were grouped by body system for analysis; conditions present in &lt;5% of the cohort were collapsed into “Other.” Functional limitations were defined using two items from the 2009– 10 National Survey of Children and Youth with Special Healthcare Needs21: “During the past 12 months (since birth if &lt;1 year), how often have your child’s health conditions affected (his/her) ability to do things other children (his/her) age do?” and “Do these conditions affect (his/her) ability to do things a great deal, some, or very little?” These items were chosen to reflect the extent of global functional limitations, and because we have previously demonstrated their association with worse pneumonia outcomes.20
