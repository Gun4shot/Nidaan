It may also be used consistent with the principles of fair use under the copyright law.Background.Evidence is limited on the comparative safety of antibiotic regimens for treatment of community-acquired pneumonia (CAP). We compared the risk of adverse drug events (ADEs) associated with antibiotic regimens for CAP treatment among otherwise healthy, nonelderly adults.Methods.We conducted an active-comparator new-user cohort study (2007–2019) of commercially insured adults aged 18–64 years diagnosed with outpatient CAP, evaluated via chest X-ray, and dispensed a same-day CAP-related oral antibiotic regimen. ADE follow-up duration ranged from 2 to 90 days (eg, renal failure [14 days]). We estimated risk differences [RDs] per 1000 treatment episodes and risk ratios using propensity score–weighted Kaplan-Meier functions. Ankle/knee sprain and influenza vaccination were considered as negative control outcomes.Results.Of 145 137 otherwise healthy CAP patients without comorbidities, 52% received narrow-spectrum regimens (44% macrolide, 8% doxycycline) and 48% received broad-spectrum regimens (39% fluoroquinolone, 7% β-lactam, 3% β-lactam + macrolide). Compared with macrolide monotherapy, each broad-spectrum antibiotic regimen was associated with increased risk of several ADEs (eg, β-lactam: nausea/vomiting/abdominal pain [RD per 1000, 3.20; 95% CI, 0.99–5.73]; non–Clostridioides difficile diarrhea [RD per 1000, 4.61; 95% CI, 2.47–6.82]; vulvovaginal candidiasis/vaginitis [RD per 1000, 3.57; 95% CI, 0.87, 6.88]). Narrow-spectrum antibiotic regimens largely conferred similar risk of ADEs. We generally observed similar risks of each negative control outcome, indicating minimal confounding.Conclusions.Broad-spectrum antibiotics were associated with increased risk of ADEs among otherwise healthy adults treated for CAP in the outpatient setting. Evidence-based clinical practice guidelines on the management of adult outpatients with CAP recommend empiric antibiotic therapy. Broad-spectrum regimens have better empiric coverage and have been recommended for patients with risk factors for antibiotic-resistant pathogens or select comorbidities [2, 3]. Narrow-spectrum regimens have been recommended for otherwise healthy patients in low-antibiotic-resistance regions. These treatment recommendations are based on efficacy and safety evidence from randomized clinical trials (RCTs), observational studies, and data on antimicrobial resistance and antibiotic spectrum of action.Knowledge about the safety of different oral antibiotic regimens is particularly relevant to inform CAP prescribing choices, given the absence of differences in efficacy. Existing studies of efficacy provide little evidence of superiority or equivalence of 1 antibiotic regimen over another [2-6]. Recent Cochrane meta-analyses of RCTs reported no difference in clinical effectiveness between different antibiotic regimens in outpatients with CAP [4, 6]. A network meta-analysis of 24 RCTs comparing empiric oral antibiotic regimens in adults with mild–moderate CAP observed trends toward a better clinical response and lower mortality for quinolones, but found no conclusive evidence of any antibiotic regimen being clearly more effective than another [7]. Non-experimental studies on effectiveness are limited and have been inconclusive due to small sample size [3]. Furthermore, clinical practice guidelines highlight the paucity of evidence on the risk of adverse events related to oral antibiotic therapy for the treatment of CAP in the outpatient setting, and call for additional research [3, 6]. Non-experimental safety studies of patients with other common infections report that non–first-line agents are frequently associated with higher risk of several individual adverse drug events (ADEs) and potential microbiome-related adverse events including non–Clostridioides difficile diarrhea, C difficile infection (CDI), vaginitis/vulvovaginal candidiasis, and more [8-10].Uncertainty about the risks and benefits of different antibiotic regimens has resulted in wide variation in antibiotic prescribing for CAP in outpatient settings [11]. For example, broad-spectrum agents (eg, fluoroquinolones) are commonly prescribed for the treatment of CAP among otherwise healthy patients in the outpatient setting. These prescribing practices are contrary to guidelines [3, 6] and antimicrobial stewardship guidance from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention to use narrow-spectrum agents when possible to treat bacterial infections [12, 13]. Using national data from a large commercial insurance database, we performed an active-comparator new-user study to estimate the comparative risk of several individual ADEs between different antibiotic regimens to treat outpatient CAP among otherwise healthy, nonelderly adults in the United States.METHODSData SourceWe used the Merative MarketScan Commercial Database (2006–2019), which includes longitudinal, patient-level data on enrollment, adjudicated inpatient and outpatient medical insurance claims, and outpatient pharmacy-dispensed medications. The database includes individuals with employer-sponsored commercial insurance and their spouses and dependents [14]. The Washington University School of Medicine Institutional Review Board deemed this study exempt from human subjects review.Study Design and PopulationWe identified a cohort of adults aged 18–64 years with an International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-9-CM), or an International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-10-CM), diagnosis code for pneumonia (Supplementary Table 1) (ignoring diagnostic/rule-out claims) diagnosed in an outpatient setting from 1 July 2007 to 30 November 2019. We required same-day treatment (index date) with a guideline-recommended oral antibiotic (see “Antibiotic Exposure” section) per the 2007 and 2019 Infectious Diseases Society of America / American Thoracic Society (IDSA/ATS) CAP guidelines [2, 3]. We also required continuous medical and prescription drug insurance enrollment during the 180-day baseline period before the index date. We implemented several exclusion criteria to create a final cohort of otherwise healthy adults with CAP that was unlikely to be caused by a multidrug-resistant pathogen (Supplementary Tables 1-6) [2, 3, 15-20]. The Supplementary Methods provide additional details on these exclusions.Antibiotic ExposureWe categorized antibiotic regimens in accordance with the 2007 and 2019 IDSA/ATS guidelines on the clinical management of adults with CAP [2, 3]. We included antibiotic regimens recommended in both guidelines (macrolide, doxycycline, respiratory fluoroquinolones, β-lactam plus macrolide, β-lactam plus doxycycline) and added β-lactam monotherapy, given common real-world prescribing for CAP. We did not include amoxicillin monotherapy in the primary analysis, as this new recommendation from the 2019 guidelines [3] occurred very late in the study period. Rather, amoxicillin initiators were included with β-lactam monotherapy in the main analysis, and further studied in subgroup analyses. We additionally categorized antibiotic regimens by coverage type, defined as narrow-spectrum (macrolide, doxycycline) or broad-spectrum (respiratory fluoroquinolones, β-lactam monotherapy, β-lactam plus macrolide, β-lactam plus doxycycline) [2]. Supplementary Table 5 presents antibiotic generic names and coverage by antibiotic regimen category.Safety OutcomesWe used ICD-9-CM and ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes to identify individual ADE outcomes, including nausea/vomiting/abdominal pain, rash/urticaria, non–C difficile diarrhea, vulvovaginal candidiasis/vaginitis, unspecified allergy, renal failure, tendinopathy (including tendon rupture), CDI, and anaphylaxis/angioedema/laryngeal edema [21]. This approach captures ADEs that are serious enough for the patient to seek medical care. Outcome-specific follow-up periods ranged from 2 to 90 days, based on expert knowledge of anticipated onset after antibiotic initiation (Supplementary Table 7) [9, 10, 22]. We also used negative-control outcomes—ankle/knee sprain and influenza vaccination—which have been used previously to assess the comparability of treatment groups (Supplementary Table 7) [9, 23]. Each negative-control outcome met the criteria of being (1) causally unrelated to the treatment under study and (2) sharing common confounding structures with the treatments and outcomes of interest (ie, the same confounders affect the negative control as the outcome of interest) [24, 25]. To ensure identification of new-onset outcomes, we excluded index events coded for the outcome of interest within 30 days before the index event date, except for influenza vaccination.CovariatesPotential confounders of the association between antibiotic exposure and ADE outcomes were identified a priori based on clinical knowledge. The following patient- and provider-level characteristics were assessed at the index event date: age, sex, geographic region, health insurance plan type, urban residence (ie, residence in a metropolitan statistical area), calendar month, calendar year, provider specialty, provider outpatient location (ie, office, emergency department, urgent care center, other), and ceftriaxone administration on the date of CAP diagnosis (since patients with CAP frequently receive a one-time dose of intravenous [IV] ceftriaxone). The presence of baseline emergency department encounters, number of physician office visits (0, 1, 2, 3+), number of unique medication therapeutic groups (0, 1, 2, 3+), and frailty markers (Supplementary Table 8) were assessed during the 180-day baseline period [26]. Comorbidities (other than those used to exclude adults with comorbidities per CAP treatment guidelines) were captured using an all-available look-back period (Supplementary Table 6) [18, 19].Statistical AnalysisBaseline covariates were summarized by antibiotic regimen. We used an as-treated approach to evaluate the association between antibiotic regimens and ADE outcomes. We estimated the weighted cumulative incidence of individual ADE outcomes using Kaplan-Meier methods. Adults were followed until the first occurrence of the ADE outcome or a censoring event (health insurance disenrollment, diagnosis of a non-CAP bacterial or viral infection, diagnosis of viral pneumonia or pneumonia due to a rare organism, influenza diagnosis or oseltamivir prescription, hospitalization, end-of-outcome-specific follow-up period, or end-of-study period on 31 December 2019). In as-treated analyses, adults are considered to be exposed to the initial treatment until changes in treatment. We censored for diagnosis of non-CAP infections as a proxy for treatment changes, given the possibility of ADE outcomes related to other infections (eg, nausea/vomiting/abdominal pain, rash/urticaria) or medication use not recorded in the database (eg, over-the-counter medication, prescription paid out-of-pocket). To account for potential confounding, we used standardized mortality ratio (SMR) weights applied to the Kaplan-Meier estimator. SMR weights standardize the covariate distribution of a comparator cohort (eg, doxycycline initiators) to a reference cohort (ie, macrolide initiators), resulting in unconfounded treatment comparisons, assuming no unmeasured confounding [27]. For each macrolide/comparator contrast, SMR weights were calculated using propensity scores (p) estimated from multivariable logistic regression, where p represents the probability of initiating macrolides versus each other antibiotic of interest, accounting for baseline covariates. Age was modeled with restricted cubic splines. We used absolute standardized mean differences to assess the balance of measured covariates between treatment groups, with values less than 0.10 considered adequate [28].
